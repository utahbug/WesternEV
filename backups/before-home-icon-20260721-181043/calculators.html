Trip-planning considerations

Use these calculators as rough baselines. Real trips can change quickly, especially in mountain states, winter weather, open desert routes, and areas with long distances between reliable chargers.

  • Elevation: Long climbs can use much more energy than flat roads. Long descents may recover some energy, but do not count on recovering everything used on the climb.
  • Wind and weather: Headwinds, snow, rain, cold temperatures, slush, and heater use can all reduce range. When conditions are rough, plan a shorter leg or a larger buffer.
  • Speed: Higher highway speeds can reduce efficiency. A slower pace may be the difference between arriving comfortably and arriving with very little charge.
  • Charger reliability: Before committing to a long leg, check the next charger, recent driver comments, connector type, access hours, and at least one backup option.
  • Remote routes: A 10% buffer may be enough near many chargers, but 20% or more can be wiser in bad weather, mountain terrain, or areas with fewer charging choices.

Power words in plain language

  • kW: Charging speed or power. A 150 kW charger can add energy faster than a 50 kW charger when the car and conditions allow it.
  • kWh: Energy amount. A battery with more usable kWh can usually store more driving range.
  • mi/kWh: Efficiency. Higher miles per kWh means the car goes farther on the same energy.
  • Percent charge: The easiest planning number for daily use. A trip buffer of 10% to 20% can make road trips less stressful.
  • Speed and wind: Faster driving and headwinds can reduce range. The calculator treats them as optional planning reductions, not exact vehicle-specific predictions.
  • Home electric rate: Utility bills usually price energy in dollars per kWh. If your rate changes by time of day, season, or usage tier, use the rate that matches when you charge or a blended estimate.
